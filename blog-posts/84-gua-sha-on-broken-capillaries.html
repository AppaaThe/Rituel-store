What Broken Capillaries Actually Are (And Aren't)

Dermatologists call them telangiectasias. They're small, dilated blood vessels — usually between 0.1 and 1 mm wide — sitting in the superficial dermis close to the skin surface. The walls of these tiny vessels have lost elasticity or been structurally damaged, so they stay visibly dilated instead of expanding and contracting normally. According to the American Academy of Dermatology, telangiectasias on the face are extremely common and often permanent without medical intervention (typically vascular laser or intense pulsed light).

They aren't "broken" in the literal sense

The phrase "broken capillaries" is misleading. The vessel isn't leaking. It's not an open wound. It's a permanent dilation of an intact micro-vessel whose wall tone is compromised. This distinction matters because it changes what can and can't make them worse. Physical trauma, thermal shock, and sustained vasodilation can all push an already-weakened vessel into a more dilated state or recruit adjacent vessels to dilate too. That's how one thread becomes a cluster.

They are not the same as flushing or rosacea redness

Flushing is transient. Rosacea erythema is diffuse and partly inflammatory. Telangiectasias are specific, visible, thread-like vessels — you can point at them. You can have all three at once (particularly in rosacea subtype 1), but they respond to different care. Our guide on gua sha redness covers the difference between normal post-treatment flushing and a bad reaction.

They're often genetic, hormonal, or environmental

Genetics account for a big share — thin fair skin inherits thinner vessel walls. Estrogen fluctuations (pregnancy, perimenopause) weaken vessel tone. UV exposure over decades is one of the most documented drivers — photoaged skin loses collagen that supports the dermal vasculature. Trauma, extreme temperatures, aggressive scrubs, steroid overuse, and repeated flushing episodes (alcohol, spicy food, emotional stress) all compound the baseline.

Why the Face Is a Hotspot

Face skin is roughly half the thickness of the skin on your back, and the central face (cheeks, nose, chin) has some of the densest superficial vasculature on the body. That's why the nose goes red first in the cold, why cheeks flush with embarrassment, and why telangiectasias almost always appear on the cheeks and nostril rims before anywhere else.

The alar crease of the nose

The little curve where your nostril meets your cheek is the single most common place for broken capillaries. Thin skin, dense vessels, frequent micro-trauma from tissue-wiping during colds. Gua sha strokes that drag across this area can unmask a cluster that was already forming subclinically.

The outer cheek and zygomatic arch

The second most common zone. Sun exposure accumulates here, and the skin is pulled taut over bone — meaning firm gua sha pressure translates almost directly to the superficial vessels with less soft tissue to absorb the force.

The Direct Answer: Can You Gua Sha At All?

Yes, with real caveats. Gua sha on broken capillaries is not automatically off-limits — but the default TikTok pressure, the standard outward-and-upward stroke pattern, and the "more is more" attitude definitely are. If you already have visible telangiectasias and you keep doing gua sha the way a viral video taught you, you will probably make them more visible over months. Not dramatically, not immediately — but measurably. The capillaries don't heal from repeated stress; they accumulate it.

The version that's safe looks different. Featherlight pressure. Generous oil. Strokes that route around visible vessels rather than over them. Short sessions, not ten-minute sculpting sprees. And a very low threshold for saying "my skin is telling me no" and stopping. If that sounds like not much of a gua sha session, you're correct — and that's exactly the point. For capillary-prone skin, the gua sha you can safely do is less intense than the one selling on social media.

5 Scenarios Where Gua Sha Is OK

These are the narrow windows where, with the right technique, we'd consider using the stone on a face with some broken capillaries.

1. A few isolated threads, no active rosacea

If you have two or three small telangiectasias on one cheek and the rest of your skin is calm — not flushing, not papular, not reactive — careful gua sha that avoids the visible vessels is usually fine. You treat the forehead, jawline, neck, and the clear cheek regions. You do not stroke over the threads themselves.

2. Post-treatment (not active) rosacea in remission

Well-controlled rosacea, no current flare, no papules, no sustained flushing. Same rule: avoid visible vessel zones, featherlight pressure everywhere, stop at the first warming sensation. Plus a green light from your dermatologist.

3. Broken capillaries on the lower face only

If the threads are on the chin or jaw margin, you have more latitude treating the upper face and neck. Skip the affected zone entirely and work in the areas that don't have the issue.

4. Scheduled vascular laser in the future (not recent past)

If your plan is vascular laser in 3+ months and you want to use gua sha carefully in the meantime, it's reasonable — again, avoiding the visible vessels. Don't try to "fix" them with the stone. The laser will.

5. Lymphatic drainage strokes on the neck only

Neck gua sha for fluid and tech-neck is very different from facial sculpting. The neck has fewer superficial facial capillaries, and the strokes are lighter and shorter. This is often the safest entry point for people with reactive facial skin. See our lymphatic drainage guide for the exact neck sequence.

4 Scenarios Where You Must Avoid Gua Sha

1. Active rosacea flare

Warm, flushed, reactive skin — skip the stone entirely. Friction on inflamed rosacea skin can extend a flare by days and recruit new capillaries to dilate. This is well-documented in the dermatology literature on rosacea trigger avoidance. The American Academy of Dermatology lists "harsh skin care" and "vigorous rubbing" as standard rosacea trigger categories.

2. New telangiectasias forming

If you've noticed new threads appearing in the last few months — especially if you're in perimenopause or a high-stress life phase — something is driving vessel dysregulation right now. Adding mechanical stress to that is a bad bet. Wait until the pattern is stable for at least 6 months before reintroducing gua sha.

3. Recent vascular laser or IPL

Post-laser skin is mid-healing. The treated vessels are closing down over 4–12 weeks, and the surrounding tissue is remodeling. Gua sha in this window can disrupt the healing vessels and may reduce your laser results. Most vascular dermatology protocols recommend no mechanical treatments (rolling, gua sha, dermaplaning) for 6–12 weeks post-procedure. Ask your treating provider.

4. Steroid-induced capillary damage

If your telangiectasias developed after prolonged topical steroid use on the face, the skin is structurally thinned and the vessels more fragile. Gua sha is contraindicated here. Rebuilding barrier takes 6–18 months of retinoid-free, steroid-free care and dermatologist supervision.

The Capillary-Safe Gua Sha Protocol

If you've read the scenarios above and concluded your situation is in the "OK with caution" camp — here's the protocol we'd run. Shorter than normal. Lighter than normal. Boring, in the best way.

  1. Patch-test day. Single light stroke on an uninvolved jaw area. Wait 24 hours. No flare? Proceed.
  2. Prep. Clean face, pat dry. Apply 4–6 drops of a well-tolerated oil. For capillary-prone skin, we like rosehip oil ($15) — the linoleic acid supports barrier, and it gives generous slip.
  3. Temperature check. The stone should be at cool room temperature — not warm from your hand. If it's summer and the room is hot, chill the stone in the fridge for 5 minutes first.
  4. Featherlight pressure only. Imagine you're writing with a pen that runs out of ink if you press too hard. If you see any skin movement with the stroke, you're pushing too hard.
  5. Route around the vessels. Do not stroke over visible telangiectasias. Plan your pattern to border them on both sides without crossing.
  6. Short strokes, 2–3 reps. Not 10. Not 15. Two or three slow passes per zone.
  7. Finish at the neck. Down-strokes along the sides of the neck toward the collarbone. This is the drainage step — lighter and safer than facial work.
  8. Total session: 3–4 minutes. Not a sculpting session. A maintenance ritual.
  9. Stop signals. Any warming, stinging, new visible redness in a previously clear area — stop. Do not finish the session.
  10. Frequency. 2–3 times a week maximum. Not daily. Not twice a day.

Pressure — the Single Biggest Risk Factor

If you take one thing from this piece: pressure is the variable that determines outcome. A light-pressure session on capillary-prone skin is neutral-to-mildly-beneficial. A medium-pressure session is neutral-to-worsening. A heavy-pressure session is definitely worsening. There is no upside to firm pressure here. None.

How to calibrate "featherlight"

Hold the stone at the edge, not the middle — that naturally reduces how much force you can apply. Use the flat side, not the comb edge. Watch the skin: if it doesn't stretch or displace with the stroke, you're in the right zone. If you can feel the stone pressing into the cheekbone, you're too heavy.

The kitchen scale test

For beginners on reactive skin, we actually recommend practicing on a kitchen scale first. Press the stone down until it reads 100–150 grams. Memorize that pressure — that's your ceiling. Healthy-skin gua sha often runs 250–400 grams. You're aiming for roughly one-third of that. Our full pressure guide walks through the calibration.

Why pressure matters mechanistically

Firmer pressure increases local intravascular pressure on already-weakened vessels. It also increases friction and heat. Both are dilators. The goal with capillary-compromised skin is to avoid any stimulus that dilates or stresses vessels further — which means lower pressure, cooler temperature, shorter sessions, and fewer reps.

Safer Alternatives: Ice Roller and Facial Roller

Here's the part most gua sha blogs skip. If your face has broken capillaries, the stone is not the best tool. Two lower-risk options do a lot of what people use gua sha for — without the mechanical shear on fragile vessels.

Cold roller (ice roller)

A BY RITUEL Rose Ice Roller ($19) or any similar stainless-steel cold roller applies gentle vasoconstriction — the opposite of the vasodilation that worsens telangiectasia. Cold rolling reduces flushing, calms inflammation, and de-puffs without lateral drag on the skin. For rosacea-prone and capillary-prone skin, it's our go-to. The catch: chronic use on very thin skin can occasionally aggravate cold-reactive flushing in some rosacea subtype 1 presentations, so start with 1-minute sessions and watch how your skin responds.

Facial roller (jade or rose quartz)

A traditional facial roller ($16) rolls rather than drags. The pressure distribution across a rolling contact is much gentler than the shear force of a gua sha stone, which means less stress on superficial vessels. For capillary-prone skin, a jade or quartz roller used with generous oil is a legitimate daily tool where a gua sha stone isn't. The "skip the stone, add the cold" recommendation

If we had to pick one alternative for someone with active broken capillaries who still wants a morning tool ritual: cold roller in the morning, rosehip oil at night, no gua sha stone until the skin pattern stabilizes for at least 6 months. That's the plan we'd run on our own face.

What the Research Actually Says

Here's where we stop handwaving and look at what the literature supports — and doesn't.

Gua sha does increase microcirculation — that's a problem here

The most-cited gua sha study (Nielsen et al., 2007, in Explore: The Journal of Science and Healing) documented a roughly 400% increase in surface microcirculation post-gua sha, sustained for about 25 minutes. For healthy skin, that's the mechanism behind the "glow." For telangiectasia-prone skin, increased microcirculation is the risk — sustained local vasodilation on already-dilated vessels is exactly what you're trying not to amplify.

Gua sha upregulates HO-1 (anti-inflammatory), which is favorable

A 2004 study by Kuo et al. in the Journal of Alternative and Complementary Medicine showed gua sha upregulates heme oxygenase-1, an anti-inflammatory enzyme. That's a genuine benefit — and part of why some rosacea patients tolerate very gentle gua sha well. But the anti-inflammatory benefit does not offset the mechanical and vasodilatory risk if pressure is not carefully controlled.

AAD on telangiectasia: mechanical trauma is a documented trigger

The American Academy of Dermatology's patient education on rosacea and visible blood vessels lists friction, heat, and repeated mechanical trauma to the face among the environmental factors that worsen facial vascular changes. Vascular laser (pulsed dye or KTP) and intense pulsed light remain the only treatments with strong evidence for actually removing telangiectasias. No topical product and no massage tool will clear them.

Barrier support reduces flushing reactivity

A 2015 clinical review (Phetcharat et al., Clinical Interventions in Aging) and earlier work in the British Journal of Dermatology (1998) on topical linoleic acid support the idea that barrier-building topicals — cold-pressed rosehip oil is a reasonable candidate — reduce transepidermal water loss and lower flushing reactivity over 4–8 weeks of consistent use. This is the slow, structural approach that actually helps capillary-prone skin. Not a massage. A long consistent topical routine.

What Gua Sha Cannot Do for Broken Capillaries

This is the part nobody wants to write because it's anti-hype. We'll write it anyway, because it's the truth.

It cannot fade existing telangiectasias

No gua sha protocol — no matter how light, how frequent, or how expertly performed — will erase broken capillaries. The vessel wall structural damage is not a massage problem. It's a vascular problem. If you want the threads gone, the evidence-based path is consultation with a board-certified dermatologist about vascular laser (pulsed dye laser, KTP) or intense pulsed light. One to three sessions typically clear most facial telangiectasias. Budget: $300–$600 per session in the US, highly variable by region.

It cannot prevent new capillaries from forming

New capillaries form because of genetics, hormones, UV damage, and sustained inflammation. A stone cannot intervene in any of those pathways. What can help: daily broad-spectrum SPF, trigger avoidance (alcohol, spicy food, extreme heat if you flush), gentle skincare, and prescription anti-rosacea therapies when indicated.

It cannot substitute for medical treatment

If you have active rosacea, you need a derm. If you have visible telangiectasias you want removed, you need a derm. Gua sha is a complementary ritual at best. It is not a treatment.

It won't "boost collagen enough to hide the vessels"

You'll read this claim. It's not supported. Mild mechanical stimulation may have modest collagen effects over months, but not enough to structurally thicken dermis to the point of hiding dilated superficial vessels. The effect is cosmetic, temporary, and small.

Red Flags — Stop and See a Derm

If any of these happen, stop gua sha entirely and book a dermatology consultation:

  • New clusters of capillaries appearing in areas you work on with the stone
  • Prolonged redness (>48 hours) after a gua sha session
  • A visible thread turning darker, thicker, or extending
  • Pustules or papules appearing in treated zones
  • Burning, stinging, or heat sensations during routine application
  • Any change that makes you wonder "is this normal?" — if you're wondering, the answer is have it checked

A single dermatology visit resolves more uncertainty than 40 hours of Googling. If you have access to care, use it early. This is especially true if you're in perimenopause, on topical steroids, or have a family history of rosacea.

FAQ

Can gua sha cause broken capillaries?

Heavy-pressure gua sha on thin, sun-damaged, or already fragile skin can contribute to new telangiectasia over months. It's rarely the sole cause — genetics, UV, and hormones do the most work — but repeated mechanical and thermal stress can accelerate vessel dilation in predisposed skin. Light pressure with generous oil is much less likely to contribute.

Will gua sha make my existing broken capillaries worse?

It can, if pressure is too firm, if you stroke directly over the visible vessels, or if you work on actively inflamed skin. Featherlight pressure, avoidance of the vessels themselves, and stopping during flares minimize the risk. There is no evidence gua sha improves existing telangiectasias.

What's the safest face tool for broken capillaries?

A cold roller (ice roller) is the safest. It causes vasoconstriction rather than vasodilation, applies no shear force, and works with rosacea and fragile skin. A facial roller is a distant second. A gua sha stone is the most demanding tool to use safely on capillary-prone skin.

Can I do gua sha after vascular laser or IPL?

Not for at least 6–12 weeks after treatment, and only with your provider's written go-ahead. The treated vessels are in a healing phase and mechanical stress can disrupt the outcome. Follow your treating dermatologist's protocol — they may say longer.

Does rosehip oil help broken capillaries?

Rosehip oil doesn't remove existing telangiectasias, but the linoleic acid content supports barrier function, which can reduce flushing reactivity over 4–8 weeks. It's a reasonable part of a capillary-prone skin routine. Use it at night, keep your daytime routine simple, and pair with daily SPF. Our rosehip oil for rosacea guide has the full protocol.

Is amethyst better than jade for fragile skin?

Stone type matters less than pressure and technique. Amethyst is smooth, dense, and stays cool — all of which help. But a jade or rose quartz stone used with featherlight pressure and good oil is also fine. A heavy hand on any stone is the issue. For more on stone choice, see our amethyst vs jade comparison.

Should I see a dermatologist before using gua sha if I have broken capillaries?

If you're unsure, yes. A single board-certified dermatology consult — even a telederm visit — gives you a personalized read on whether your specific vascular pattern is safe for mechanical tools. It's $100–$300 well spent if it saves your face from 12 months of the wrong routine.
