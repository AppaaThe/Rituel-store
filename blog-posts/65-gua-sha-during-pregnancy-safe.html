Is Gua Sha Safe During Pregnancy? Yes, Face Only (3 Rules)

Yes — gua sha during pregnancy is safe when you stick to three rules: face and neck only, light pressure, and avoid two specific acupressure points (SP6 on the inner ankle and LI4 in the thumb-index web). Body gua sha is a different story. Most articles you'll find online either say "absolutely fine" without any nuance, or "avoid entirely" with no reasoning. The honest answer lives in the middle, and it depends on which trimester you're in, which area of the body you're working on, and a few specific anatomical pressure points that traditional acupressure has documented as contraindicated during pregnancy for centuries. This guide is written cautiously — because when you're pregnant, caution is the right posture — but not alarmist, because the truth is that facial gua sha is low-risk when done with awareness.

Before going further, one thing: nothing in this guide replaces a conversation with your own obstetrician or midwife. What follows is a synthesis of available research and traditional Chinese medicine guidance, but your individual pregnancy has variables we can't see. If you have a high-risk pregnancy, a history of miscarriage, placenta previa, or anything else that makes your provider cautious, bring this conversation to them before you start.

Key takeaway:

Facial gua sha is generally safe during pregnancy when done on the face and neck only, with light pressure, avoiding the specific acupressure points Spleen 6 (SP6) on the inner ankle and Large Intestine 4 (LI4) in the web between thumb and index finger. Skip body gua sha, abdominal work, and firm-pressure lymphatic drainage on the lower legs entirely. Always clear with your OB first, especially if high-risk or first trimester.

The Short Answer (And Why It's Nuanced)

Facial gua sha is low-risk during pregnancy. Body gua sha and specific acupressure points have documented risk. The confusion online comes from conflating the two practices. Both use the word "gua sha" but they're different modalities. Facial gua sha is a light massage with a stone tool on the face and neck. Traditional body gua sha involves firm scraping on the back, shoulders, and limbs to produce intentional petechiae — and traditional acupuncture and acupressure sources have listed certain points as contraindicated during pregnancy for centuries.

The two pressure points that show up across every traditional source as contraindicated are Spleen 6 (SP6) — located four finger-widths above the inner ankle bone — and Large Intestine 4 (LI4) — located in the fleshy web between your thumb and index finger. A 2004 paper in the Journal of Alternative and Complementary Medicine and multiple obstetric acupuncture texts describe these points as potentially contributing to uterine contraction when stimulated strongly. You won't encounter either in a face gua sha routine. But if you do full-body work or hand/foot massage, you need to know where they are.

Trimester-by-Trimester Guidance

First trimester (weeks 1–13)

This is the most cautious window. Miscarriage risk is highest, and even though facial gua sha doesn't meaningfully contribute to that risk, the general principle in pregnancy care is conservatism when uncertain. The conservative guidance many midwives recommend is to skip gua sha entirely for the first 12 weeks, or limit strictly to very light face and neck work if you want to continue. If you've been doing it daily pre-pregnancy, it's fine to continue. If you're thinking of starting for the first time, wait until the second trimester.

Second trimester (weeks 14–27)

The window where most practitioners agree facial gua sha is safe and actually beneficial. Pregnancy-related fluid retention is real, your face and neck can puff, and gentle lymphatic drainage work helps. Use light-to-medium pressure. Stick to face and neck only. Skip any vigorous body work.

Third trimester (weeks 28–40)

Continue face and neck work if it's been working for you. Caution on anything that puts you in an uncomfortable position — your circulation is different now, and lying flat on your back for extended periods can restrict blood flow to the baby (supine hypotensive syndrome). Sit upright or reclined at an angle. Keep sessions short — 3–5 minutes is plenty. Swelling in the lower legs during this trimester is often a medical signal (preeclampsia risk) rather than something to "drain" — do not attempt leg gua sha during pregnancy.

Pressure Points to Avoid Entirely

Spleen 6 (SP6)

Location: four finger-widths (about 3 inches) above the prominent inner ankle bone, on the back edge of the tibia. This point is used in labor induction protocols in some traditional practices. Firm, sustained stimulation is contraindicated throughout pregnancy. Incidental contact when putting on socks is not a problem — we're talking about intentional pressure work.

Large Intestine 4 (LI4)

Location: in the web between your thumb and index finger, at the highest point when you bring thumb and finger together. Also used in some traditional induction protocols. Firm pressure is contraindicated. Again — shaking someone's hand isn't the concern. Intentional sustained pressure is.

Sacrum and lower back

The bladder meridian runs along the lower back, and several points in this area are traditionally cautioned during pregnancy. More practically, firm scraping in this area can cause discomfort and is just not necessary during pregnancy. Skip.

Abdomen

Obvious but worth stating: no gua sha on the abdomen at any point during pregnancy. This is non-negotiable across all sources.

Deep calf work

Pregnancy increases DVT (deep vein thrombosis) risk, particularly in the third trimester. Deep-pressure work on the calves is not recommended. If your legs are swollen, elevate them and walk — don't scrape.

Face Gua Sha During Pregnancy — What's Safe

The face is generally clear of pregnancy-contraindicated acupressure points. The techniques we recommend for facial depuffing, tension release, and lymphatic drainage are low-force, surface-level movements that don't stimulate any of the points traditional sources flag. The standard 5-minute morning routine is safe in second and third trimesters with one modification: reduce pressure by about 30% compared to non-pregnancy. Err light, always.

Pregnancy-specific facial concerns gua sha can help with

Pregnancy puffiness: Increased blood volume and fluid retention during pregnancy often causes facial and under-eye puffiness, especially in the mornings. Light lymphatic drainage on face and neck is genuinely helpful here.

Pregnancy tension headaches: Hormone shifts, poor sleep, and posture changes contribute to frequent tension headaches. Gentle jaw and temple work can provide meaningful relief. See our gua sha for headaches piece for the technique, with the caveat to use the pregnancy-lighter pressure.

Melasma concerns: The "mask of pregnancy" affects many women. Gua sha won't fade melasma directly, but the improved circulation supports overall skin health. Pair with strict daily SPF.

Why Body Gua Sha Is Different (And Riskier)

Body gua sha is a therapeutic modality distinct from facial gua sha. It's performed by trained practitioners using firm scraping on large muscle groups, intentionally producing petechiae (the signature red-purple marks) as a manifestation of circulatory release. During pregnancy, this isn't appropriate for several reasons:

  • Mechanical forces are significantly greater than facial work
  • Many of the contraindicated acupressure points live on the body
  • Pregnancy-induced hypercoagulability increases bruising and bleeding risk
  • Pressure on certain reflex zones has documented traditional contraindications

If you're looking for body relief during pregnancy — back pain, leg cramps, general tension — pregnancy-certified prenatal massage therapists exist. They're trained in what positions are safe (side-lying, supported positioning), what pressure points to avoid, and how to modify technique for different trimesters. That's the appropriate referral. Don't try to improvise body gua sha at home during pregnancy.

The Oil Matters Too — What's Pregnancy-Safe

Your slip oil needs to be pregnancy-safe. Most plant oils are fine, but a few aren't.

Safe: Rosehip seed oil, jojoba, squalane, sweet almond, grapeseed, sunflower.

Use caution / consult provider: Essential oil blends (some essential oils have emmenagogue properties), retinol-containing facial oils (topical retinoids are contraindicated during pregnancy), any oil with added "actives" like bakuchiol or vitamin C unless cleared by your OB.

Rosehip oil naturally contains small amounts of trans-retinoic acid, which is structurally related to retinoids. The amounts are low and most obstetric dermatology references consider plain rosehip oil acceptable during pregnancy, but this is a reasonable conversation to have with your provider. If you're avoiding all retinoids during pregnancy out of caution (a defensible choice), switch to pure jojoba or squalane as your slip oil for the duration.

Signs to Stop Immediately

Stop gua sha and contact your obstetric provider if you experience:

  • Any uterine contractions or cramping during or after sessions
  • Unusual bruising pattern that doesn't match the pressure you used
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness during a session
  • Any unexpected bleeding
  • Sudden or new facial swelling (could be preeclampsia — medical evaluation urgent)

None of these are common with facial gua sha done at the recommended pressure, but pregnancy care is always better safe than sorry.

What Gua Sha Cannot Do During Pregnancy

  • Reverse melasma or the "mask of pregnancy" — hormonal pigmentation requires actives and SPF
  • Induce labor (at the points we recommend — face and neck work doesn't)
  • Reduce pregnancy-related weight gain in the face — that's hormonal, not lymphatic
  • Treat pre-eclamptic swelling — swelling with medical concern requires medical evaluation
  • Replace prenatal massage for body pain — see a certified prenatal therapist

Is gua sha during pregnancy safe? The complete verdict

To answer the exact query directly: yes, gua sha during pregnancy is safe — but only on the face and neck, with light pressure, and only if you avoid the SP6 and LI4 acupressure points. Body gua sha is not safe during pregnancy and should be skipped entirely. That's the short version. Here's why the answer has those qualifiers.

Facial gua sha is a low-force surface treatment. The pressure used (about 200–500 grams, roughly the weight of an apple) doesn't reach depths where it could affect uterine activity. The face also doesn't contain any of the acupressure points that traditional Chinese medicine flags as contraindicated during pregnancy. So when you stick to face and neck, the practice is mechanically and energetically safe.

Body gua sha is different. The pressure used is several times higher (intentionally producing petechiae), the modality stimulates large muscle groups and meridian lines that include flagged points, and pregnancy-induced changes in clotting make bruising and bleeding less predictable. Combine those three factors and the risk-benefit math doesn't work — you don't get a benefit during pregnancy that you can't get from prenatal massage instead, and you do take on risk that isn't worth it.

The two specific points to avoid — Spleen 6 and Large Intestine 4 — are documented across traditional sources as having uterine effects when stimulated with sustained, firm pressure. They're listed in modern obstetric acupuncture induction protocols. Incidental contact (putting on socks, shaking hands) is not a risk; intentional pressure work is. Neither point is anywhere near the face, so a facial routine doesn't go near them. Just don't do hand or ankle work during pregnancy.

One more clarifying point: this guidance is the same whether you're 6 weeks or 36 weeks. The areas to avoid don't change. What changes by trimester is the level of overall caution — first trimester errs more conservative, second trimester is the most permissive window, third trimester adds positioning concerns (no extended supine lying). All three trimesters share the same "face only, light pressure, avoid SP6 + LI4" rule.

FAQ

Can I gua sha my face in the first trimester?

If you've been doing it pre-pregnancy, yes — with lighter pressure. If you're starting new, most practitioners recommend waiting until the second trimester as a conservative default. Always discuss with your OB first.

Can gua sha induce labor?

Face and neck work — no. Stimulation of specific acupressure points like SP6 and LI4 with firm sustained pressure — there's traditional evidence those points are used in induction, but modern research is limited. Avoiding those specific points is reasonable caution.

Is rosehip oil safe during pregnancy?

Generally yes. It naturally contains trace trans-retinoic acid, which some providers advise avoiding out of abundant caution. If your OB has asked you to avoid all retinoids, switch to jojoba or squalane during pregnancy.

Can I use gua sha for pregnancy-related puffiness?

Yes, on face and neck only. Light pressure. Skip if you have sudden or dramatic swelling, which needs medical evaluation.

Is it safe while breastfeeding?

Yes. Breastfeeding doesn't carry the acupressure-point contraindications of pregnancy. Standard routine with any safe slip oil is fine.
