Before You Start — 3 Things to Know

Do not gua sha on dry skin. Do not gua sha on a freshly cleansed but bone-dry face. The stone needs slip, and your skin needs a lubricant layer to prevent micro-tearing. Apply 3–5 drops of facial oil and spread it evenly across your face and neck. Rosehip seed oil is our default because it's non-comedogenic and rich in linoleic acid — see our breakdown of whether rosehip oil clogs pores if you're worried about breakouts.

Second: sit in front of a mirror for your first five sessions. You'll catch mistakes in angle and direction you can't feel. After ten sessions, you can do it with your eyes closed during your morning shower routine.

Third: the point is not to bruise yourself. Gua sha for the face is distinct from gua sha for the back or shoulders. On the body, the technique creates intentional petechiae (the red marks the practice is known for). On the face, those marks mean you've pressed too hard. Medium-light pressure, always. If you're leaving streaks, you're overdoing it. Our pressure guide has a visual pressure scale.

How to Hold the Stone (The 15-Degree Rule)

This is the single detail that separates good technique from bad technique: the stone meets your skin at a 15-degree angle. Not flat. Not perpendicular. Fifteen degrees. Imagine the stone lying face-down on a table — now tilt the leading edge up slightly so only the bottom edge makes skin contact. That tilt is 15 degrees. It's a gentle glide, not a scrape.

Hold the stone with your thumb on the concave (curved-in) side and your index and middle finger on the convex (curved-out) side. Grip is firm but not tense. Your wrist does most of the work — the stone is an extension of your hand, not a tool you drag across your face.

Most beginner shapes have a rounded edge, a long concave edge, a heart-notch edge, and a comb edge. For this beginner routine, you'll use primarily the long concave edge and the rounded edge. We'll leave the heart notch and comb for more advanced work once you've locked in the fundamentals. Gua Sha Strokes for Beginners: The 7 Moves in Order

Do these in exactly this order. The logic is anatomical — you're draining your face from the bottom of the lymphatic chain upward, opening the drainage pathways before you send more fluid toward them. Starting anywhere else just moves fluid into closed channels and can cause puffiness to sit rather than clear.

Stroke 1 — Neck flush (prepare the drain)

Place the long concave edge of the stone at the base of your ear. Glide downward along the side of your neck toward your collarbone. Lift the stone, reset at the ear, and repeat. This opens the cervical lymphatic chain and the supraclavicular nodes at your collarbone, which is where all head-and-neck lymphatic fluid exits. Without this first, every other stroke has nowhere to drain. 5 strokes each side.

Stroke 2 — Jawline sweep (lymph node activation)

Start at the center of your chin with the concave edge. Glide upward and outward along your jawline to the base of your ear. This runs the stone along the submandibular lymph nodes. Keep the stone flat against the jawbone and use your non-stone hand to gently pull the skin above your jaw taut for better contact. 5 strokes each side.

Stroke 3 — Cheek lift (the first visible stroke)

Start at the side of your nose with the concave edge. Glide outward along your cheekbone to your temple. Lift at the temple. This is the stroke people see working — it creates the subtle "lift" look that makes your face appear more sculpted immediately after. The outward direction moves fluid toward the pre-auricular nodes just in front of your ears. 5 strokes each side.

Stroke 4 — Under-eye fan (lightest pressure on the face)

Use the rounded edge for this. The skin under your eye is the thinnest on your body — lighter pressure than anywhere else. Start at the inner corner, glide outward and slightly upward in a gentle fan toward the temple. No scooping. No pressing into the tear trough. Just a gentle glide. This reduces morning under-eye puffiness more than any other single stroke. 3 strokes each side.

Stroke 5 — Brow lift

Switch back to the long concave edge. Place it at the inner edge of your brow, right where it meets the bridge of your nose. Glide outward along and slightly above the brow bone toward the temple. This is the stroke that wakes your eyes up. Do this slowly — fast doesn't work here. 5 strokes each side.

Stroke 6 — Forehead sweep

Start at the center of your forehead, just above your brows. Sweep outward and slightly upward toward your temples. Then do a second pass starting higher, closer to your hairline. Two rows. This opens the frontal and temporal drainage and softens tension lines. 5 strokes each side, both rows.

Stroke 7 — Final drainage (close the circuit)

Repeat Stroke 1 — the neck flush. Five strokes down each side. This "closes" the circuit by pushing all the fluid you just moved down and out through the collarbone. Skipping this is the most common mistake beginners make, and it's why some people feel puffier after gua sha rather than less. 5 strokes each side.

Stroke Counts and Pressure by Zone

Quick reference for the whole routine:

  • Neck flush — 5 strokes each side, medium pressure
  • Jawline sweep — 5 strokes each side, medium pressure
  • Cheek lift — 5 strokes each side, medium-light pressure
  • Under-eye fan — 3 strokes each side, very light pressure
  • Brow lift — 5 strokes each side, light pressure
  • Forehead sweep — 5 strokes each side, both rows, medium pressure
  • Final drainage — 5 strokes each side, medium pressure

Total: about 76 strokes, 5 minutes at a steady pace. Cited research from Nielsen et al., 2007 in Explore: The Journal of Science and Healing documented a roughly 400% increase in surface microcirculation following gua sha, persisting about 25 minutes — the window in which you're getting the real circulatory benefit of the session.

The 4 Mistakes Every Beginner Makes

Mistake 1 — Stroking in both directions

Gua sha is directional. Every stroke goes one way. You lift the stone at the end and reset to the starting position. Dragging back and forth negates the lymphatic drainage effect — you're pushing fluid around in circles rather than moving it toward exit points.

Mistake 2 — Using the stone flat against skin

Flat contact creates more friction and less glide. The 15-degree angle is not optional. Practice this in a mirror for your first three sessions until it becomes automatic.

Mistake 3 — Skipping the final drainage

Already mentioned above, but it bears repeating. If you only remember one thing from this guide, remember to do the neck flush at the beginning AND at the end. The routine is a circuit.

Mistake 4 — Pressing harder when not seeing results in week 1

You won't see dramatic results in week 1. That's normal. Pressing harder doesn't speed anything up — it irritates your skin. Consistency is the variable that matters. Your First 14 Days (What to Expect)

Days 1–3: Awkward. You'll feel slow, uncertain about the angle, and unsure whether you're doing anything. This is normal. Don't quit.

Days 4–7: The sequence starts to feel natural. You'll notice a pinker, warmer face immediately after sessions — that's the circulation boost.

Days 8–14: Morning puffiness starts reducing. This is the first visible change. Photos taken at day 14 vs. day 1 often show measurable difference in facial fluid distribution.

Days 15+: Tone and texture refinement begin. This is slower — plan on 4–6 weeks for clearly visible changes in tension lines and skin clarity.

What to Skip Until You're More Advanced

Don't worry about these yet — they're for months 2 and 3 of your practice:

  • The heart-notch edge for chin and cupid's bow work
  • The comb edge for forehead tension lines
  • Buccal (inside-the-mouth) technique
  • Pressure point work around the TMJ — see our TMJ guide when ready
  • Warming or cooling the stone for specific effects

The seven beginner strokes cover 90% of the benefit of a full gua sha practice. Master them first. Add complexity only once the basics are automatic.

FAQ

How long should my first session take?

10–15 minutes. By session five, you'll be at 5 minutes. Speed comes with repetition, not effort.

Can I do this in the shower?

Yes, and many people find it easier because the oil spreads beautifully and the warmth amplifies circulation. Just keep the stone clean (see our care guide).

Morning or night — which is better for beginners?

Morning, for depuffing and the visible lift effect. Night practice is also valuable but the morning benefits are more immediate and motivating for a new practice.

What if I can't find the angle?

Hold the stone against a table and tilt the leading edge up about a thumb's width off the surface. Memorize that angle visually. That's 15 degrees.

Do I need to do all 7 strokes every day?

Yes, if you want the drainage benefit. Partial routines break the circuit. A full 5-minute session beats a "focused" 2-minute session every time.
