Is Rosehip Oil Comedogenic? The Real Answer (With Science)

Short answer: no. Cold-pressed rosehip seed oil rates 1 on the comedogenic scale (the scale goes 0 to 5, with 0 being no clogging risk at all). It's one of the few face oils that's genuinely safe for acne-prone skin, because it's high in linoleic acid — the fatty acid that acne-prone skin is usually deficient in. But there are three situations where rosehip oil can still cause breakouts, and they have nothing to do with the oil itself being comedogenic. Here's the full picture.

What "comedogenic" actually means

A comedogenic ingredient is one that tends to clog pores, trapping dead skin cells and sebum inside the follicle, which then becomes a comedo — a blackhead or whitehead. Over time those can inflame into full pimples.

The comedogenic scale is 0 to 5:

  • 0: zero clogging risk (argan, hemp seed, safflower)
  • 1: very low risk (rosehip, sunflower)
  • 2: low-moderate (jojoba, olive)
  • 3: moderate (avocado)
  • 4: high (coconut oil)
  • 5: very high (cocoa butter, isopropyl myristate)

Rosehip oil at 1 is in the safest tier. For context, coconut oil (which marketing often sells as "natural and safe") rates 4 and is a well-documented breakout trigger for acne-prone skin.

Why rosehip oil is actually good for acne-prone skin

This is the part most skincare articles miss. Rosehip oil isn't just neutral for acne — it's actively helpful. The reason is its fatty acid profile.

Healthy skin sebum is roughly 15–20% linoleic acid. Acne-prone skin sebum is often below 5%. That deficiency makes sebum thicker, stickier, and more likely to plug pores. Topping up linoleic acid from outside has been shown in multiple studies to thin the sebum and reduce blackhead formation.

Rosehip seed oil is 44% linoleic acid — one of the highest of any plant oil. Applying it directly feeds the exact fatty acid your skin is short on. Combine that with its natural trans-retinoic acid (the same molecule as tretinoin, just milder) and you have an oil that's essentially a gentle acne treatment in disguise.

The three reasons rosehip oil might still break you out

1. It's oxidized

Rosehip oil is delicate. Its high polyunsaturated fat content makes it prone to oxidation — which means it goes rancid if exposed to light, heat, or air. Oxidized oil is inflammatory, sticky, and can clog pores even though fresh rosehip wouldn't.

Signs your rosehip oil is oxidized:

  • Smells like old fish or crayons (fresh rosehip has a mild earthy-grassy smell)
  • Darkened from orange-gold to dark red-brown
  • Feels tackier than it used to

This is why real rosehip oil should come in dark amber or violet glass, never clear glass or plastic, and why you should use it up within 6 months of opening. Refrigeration after opening extends the life.

2. It's refined, not cold-pressed

Cheap rosehip oils are solvent-extracted and refined, which destroys the natural retinoic acid and damages the fatty acid profile. The end product is more likely to be heavy, slower to absorb, and more likely to sit in pores. Cold-pressed rosehip seed oil is the only kind worth buying.

3. You're using too much, or over other comedogenic products

Rosehip oil isn't comedogenic on its own, but if you're layering it over a heavy silicone primer, a coconut-oil-based cleanser residue, or a pore-clogging sunscreen, the combination can suffocate acne-prone skin. The oil gets blamed; the real culprit is what's underneath.

How to patch test rosehip oil if you're nervous

If you have reactive or acne-prone skin and you want to be careful, do a proper patch test before committing.

  1. Apply 1 drop of rosehip oil to the same spot on your jawline every night for 5 nights.
  2. Don't change anything else in your routine during that time.
  3. On day 6, check the patch area vs the rest of your face.

If nothing changed, it's safe for you. If the patch area is bumpy, congested, or inflamed, you're in the very small minority who don't tolerate it — either because of an oxidation issue, a sensitivity to something else in the bottle (if it's not single-ingredient), or a rare allergy to rosa canina.

What about fungal acne?

Fungal acne (malassezia folliculitis) looks like tiny uniform bumps on the forehead, temples, or chest. It's caused by yeast, not bacteria, and it's fed by certain fatty acids — specifically oleic acid and medium-chain triglycerides.

Rosehip oil contains some oleic acid (about 14%), which could theoretically feed fungal acne in people who are prone to it. If your bumps are small, itchy, and uniform — not red, inflamed pimples — rosehip might not be the right oil for you. Squalane or MCT-free hemp seed oil is safer in that case.

The bottom line

Rosehip seed oil is non-comedogenic for the vast majority of people, including most of those with acne-prone or congested skin. It's one of the few oils that can genuinely help acne by fixing the underlying linoleic acid deficiency. If you break out from rosehip oil, the cause is usually oxidation, poor quality, or fungal acne — not the oil itself.

FAQ

Can rosehip oil cause blackheads?

Not typically. Fresh, cold-pressed rosehip seed oil is rated 1 on the comedogenic scale and is low-risk for blackheads. Oxidized or refined rosehip oil can contribute to congestion.

Is rosehip oil good for acne-prone skin?

Yes, and it's one of the best face oils specifically for acne-prone skin because of its high linoleic acid content. See our full guide on rosehip oil for acne scars for how to use it to fade marks.

How do I know if rosehip oil is breaking me out?

Pause the oil for 2 weeks and see if things clear. Then reintroduce it alone (no other new products) and watch closely for 2 more weeks. If you break out only with the oil in the routine, it's the oil — usually because of oxidation or a secondary ingredient.

Is rosehip oil safe every day?

Yes. 2–3 drops every night is the standard use. Some people use it morning and night, which is fine if you're not experiencing any congestion and you're wearing SPF in the morning.

What's the comedogenic rating of rosehip seed vs rosehip fruit oil?

Rosehip seed oil rates 1. Rosehip fruit oil is slightly higher and less well-studied. Always buy cold-pressed seed oil specifically.

Can I use rosehip oil with benzoyl peroxide or salicylic acid?

Yes, but not in the same step. Apply your acid or benzoyl peroxide first, wait for it to fully absorb, then layer rosehip oil as the buffering/repair step. This is actually a great combo because rosehip calms the irritation those actives cause.
