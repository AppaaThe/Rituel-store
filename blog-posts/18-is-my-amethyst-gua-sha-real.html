Is My Amethyst Gua Sha Real? 6 Ways to Spot a Fake

Short answer: most cheap "amethyst" gua sha sold on Amazon, Etsy, and dropship Instagram ads are dyed glass, purple resin, or soft serpentine painted to look like amethyst. Real amethyst is Mohs 7 hardness, dense, stays cold, and has subtle natural color variation. If your stone is feather-light, warms up in your hand in five seconds, or has a perfectly uniform color, it's almost certainly fake. Here are the six tests we use to tell in under a minute.

Why this matters for results

A fake amethyst gua sha doesn't just feel wrong — it actively works worse. Real amethyst polishes to a glass-smooth surface that glides over oiled skin; fakes are rougher and drag, which is the number one reason beginners end up with red streaks after their first session. Real amethyst also holds cold for the full 5-minute routine; fakes warm up in seconds, which kills the de-puffing effect. You're paying for a tool that works. If it doesn't work, it doesn't matter how pretty it looks.

Test 1: The weight test (10 seconds)

Hold your gua sha flat in your palm and just feel it. Real amethyst is dense — heavy for its size. A 70–90 gram palm-sized stone is normal. If your stone feels light, almost plasticky in hand, it's likely resin or low-density glass. Resin gua sha usually weighs 30–50% less than real stone for the same size.

Verdict: If it feels suspiciously light, it probably is.

Test 2: The cold test (60 seconds)

Put your stone on the kitchen counter for an hour at room temperature, then press it flat against your cheek. Count how long it takes to warm up to skin temperature.

  • Real amethyst: stays noticeably cold for 20–40 seconds before it starts to feel neutral.
  • Resin or glass: feels cold for 3–8 seconds, then matches your skin temperature.
  • Soft serpentine (dyed "jade" or fake amethyst): somewhere in between, but tends to feel "wet cold" instead of "crystal cold."

This is the single most useful test. It's fast, it's free, and the difference is unambiguous once you've felt both.

Test 3: The color variation test (eyeball it)

Real amethyst has natural variation. Hold the stone up to daylight and look at it carefully. You should see:

  • Deeper purple in the thicker parts of the stone
  • Lighter purple or near-clear areas near the edges
  • Sometimes thin streaks of white quartz running through
  • Subtle "banding" — zones of slightly different purple tones

A stone that's perfectly uniform purple, edge-to-edge, with no variation at all, is almost always dyed. Real amethyst color comes from iron impurities in the crystal lattice and those impurities aren't evenly distributed. If nature made it, it's not perfect.

Exception: very high-grade amethyst can be fairly uniform, but then it's also expensive and rare. If you paid €15 for a "uniform deep purple" gua sha, it's not high grade anything.

Test 4: The scratch test (only if you're sure)

Amethyst is Mohs hardness 7. Glass is around Mohs 5.5. That means real amethyst will scratch a piece of window glass, and fake glass-based stones won't.

Find a junk glass bottle or a piece of scrap glass, hold it steady on a flat surface, and drag a corner of your gua sha across it firmly. Real amethyst leaves a visible scratch on the glass. A glass "amethyst" won't leave a scratch (or will scratch itself instead).

Warning: don't do this on anything you care about, and don't do it on the main working edge of your stone. Pick a corner that doesn't touch your skin during routine use.

Test 5: The polish test (run your fingertip)

Run your fingertip slowly across the curved edge that touches your face during use. It should feel glass-smooth — no grit, no ridges, no resistance. Real hand-polished amethyst has a finish that feels almost like wet glass.

Fakes usually fail here in two ways:

  • Too rough: you can feel tiny grit or lines. This is soft serpentine or low-grade stone that wasn't fully polished. It will drag on your skin.
  • Too smooth in a plastic way: the surface feels slippery but "warm" and slightly soft. This is resin. It will hold cold for almost no time.

Real stone feels cool and hard and completely smooth — a specific combination plastic can't fake.

Test 6: The tap test (use another stone)

If you have another gua sha — any other real crystal tool — tap the two together very gently. Real amethyst rings with a hard, high "click." Resin and glass thud. It's a small difference but once you've heard both you won't forget it.

If you only have one stone, tap it gently against a ceramic coffee mug. Same principle: real stone rings, resin goes "thunk."

What to do if your amethyst gua sha is fake

First, don't panic — you haven't wasted that much money. But you should consider replacing it because:

  1. Fake stones can cause red marks and broken capillaries from the rougher surface
  2. You won't get the cold benefit that makes the routine actually work
  3. Most fakes will chip, flake, or discolor within a few months
  4. Some resin fakes leach dye onto oiled skin (we've seen it)

If you bought from a marketplace with a return window, return it. If you bought from a dropship ad and the return window is "impossible," chalk it up to a €10 lesson and buy real next time.

Red flags before you buy

Sometimes you can spot a fake before it even ships. Watch for these in listings:

  • Price under €10. Real hand-polished amethyst can't be produced at that price. It's fake or it's cheap serpentine.
  • Perfectly uniform color in the photos. Real amethyst has variation. Photos of "impossibly perfect" purple are usually dyed or AI-generated stock.
  • Same photo used on 30 different brand listings. Check Google Image reverse search. Dropshippers all share the same stock imagery.
  • Vague "premium quality stone" language with no origin. Honest sellers say where the stone comes from. Fake sellers say "high quality" and nothing else.
  • No clear return policy. Legitimate brands stand behind the product.
  • Only sold as part of a "10-piece facial tool set" for €15. Nothing in that box is real.

FAQ

Can an amethyst gua sha be natural but still bad quality?

Yes. Low-grade amethyst exists — it's real stone but poorly polished, cracked, or carved too thin. These will break quickly even if they pass the "is it real" tests. You want real and well-made.

Does real amethyst always feel cold?

Not always cold to the touch, but always cooler than your skin and always slower to warm up than resin or glass. The test is how long it holds the cold, not the starting temperature.

Is my amethyst gua sha real if it's very dark purple?

It can be. Deep "royal purple" amethyst is real and valuable. But deep color with no variation, at a low price, is almost always dyed.

What's the easiest single test to tell if my amethyst gua sha is real?

The cold test. Leave it on the counter, press it to your cheek, count seconds. Real amethyst stays cold much longer than fakes. No equipment needed.

Should I worry about using a fake amethyst gua sha on my skin?

Mostly yes. Fake stones drag more, which can cause broken capillaries. Some resin fakes also leach dye. We'd rather you replace a fake than push through with it.
