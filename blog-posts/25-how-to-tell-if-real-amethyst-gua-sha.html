How to Tell If Your Amethyst Gua Sha Is Real: 6 Tests You Can Do at Home

The short answer

If you want to know whether your amethyst gua sha is real in under a minute, do the temperature test: press the flat side against your cheek. Real amethyst stays noticeably cool for 8–10 seconds before it warms up — dyed glass and resin reach body temperature almost instantly. Follow that with a quick color check (real amethyst has uneven purple gradients, never a perfectly uniform lilac) and a scratch test against a glass bottle (real amethyst sits at Mohs 7 and will scratch glass; glass fakes won’t). If the tool was under $10, you’re almost certainly holding dyed glass. We sell real amethyst, so below we’ll walk you through all six tests the way we run them on our own intake table.

Why authenticity actually matters (it’s not just energy)

We’ll leave the crystal metaphysics for another post. The practical reason you want real amethyst on your face is mechanical: genuine quartz-family stone is polished to a continuous, non-porous surface that glides over serum without dragging. Dyed glass chips at the edges and develops microscopic burrs. Purple resin can shed plasticizers onto warm, freshly-cleansed skin. Dyed quartzite (a common fake) is porous, meaning it absorbs your oil cleanser and grows bacteria in the grain.

The worst-case scenario isn’t that your tool “doesn’t work” — it’s that a chipped glass edge opens a micro-cut along your jawline while you’re trying to de-puff. We see photos of this in our customer-service inbox every week, and they’re almost always from tools bought on marketplaces for under $8 shipped.

The 6 ways to check if your amethyst gua sha is real

1. The temperature test (the fastest tell)

Real amethyst is a natural heat sink. Its crystalline lattice conducts heat away from the contact point, which is why gua sha feels cold even after sitting on your vanity at room temperature. Hold the stone flat against the inside of your wrist for a full ten seconds.

  • Real amethyst: stays cool for 8–10 seconds, warms slowly, cools back down within 30 seconds of release.
  • Dyed glass: feels cool for roughly 2 seconds, then matches your skin temperature.
  • Resin: already room-temperature warm when you pick it up. This one is obvious.

If your tool fails this test, you can stop here. Nothing else will save it.

2. The weight test

Amethyst is a variety of quartz with a density of roughly 2.65 g/cm³. Soda-lime glass (the most common fake material) lands around 2.5 g/cm³, and acrylic resin is closer to 1.2 g/cm³. That means a real amethyst gua sha of standard heart shape — about 8 cm wide — should weigh between 75 and 110 grams. Resin fakes often weigh less than 40 grams. If the piece feels suspiciously light, like a kid’s plastic toy, it is one.

No kitchen scale? Hold a real stone tool in one hand and your suspect piece in the other. You’ll feel the difference immediately. Real stone has what jewelers call “heft” — a dense, settled weight that plastic can’t fake.

3. The color check (look for gradient, not perfection)

This is where mass-market fakes betray themselves. Natural amethyst grows in geodes under uneven pressure and heat, so the color distribution is never uniform. Real amethyst shows a spectrum from pale lavender to deep violet within a single piece, usually with visible zoning — bands, clouds, or a darker “tip” where the crystal terminated.

Dyed glass is the opposite. It’s a flat, Pantone-perfect purple from edge to edge, sometimes with a faint greenish undertone when you tilt it under warm light. Dyed quartzite fakes show color pooling in the cracks where the dye settled — a dead giveaway if you run the stone under a desk lamp.

As a rule: if it looks too pretty, too even, too Instagram — it’s probably fake. Real amethyst has character. We cover this in more detail in our guide on rose quartz vs amethyst gua sha, where uneven coloring separates the real ones in both stones.

4. The bubble test

Hold the gua sha up to a bright window or phone flashlight. Tilt it slowly and look into the stone, not at its surface.

  • Real amethyst may contain natural inclusions — tiny threads, cloudy veils, or small fractures called “gardens.” It will never contain perfectly round air bubbles.
  • Glass fakes almost always contain at least one or two spherical bubbles trapped during the molding process. They look like perfect little pinheads suspended in the material.

One visible round bubble equals glass. Full stop. Quartz does not form bubbles.

5. The scratch test (use a glass jar, not your bathroom mirror)

Amethyst registers 7 on the Mohs hardness scale. Ordinary soda-lime glass is between 5.5 and 6. That means real amethyst will scratch glass, but glass cannot scratch amethyst. Find an old glass bottle or jam jar you don’t care about, and drag the thin edge of your gua sha firmly across the shoulder of the bottle.

  • If a visible, permanent scratch appears on the glass — the stone is real (or at least genuine quartz).
  • If the edge of the gua sha dulls or chips instead — it’s glass hitting glass, and you have a fake.

Do this on an inconspicuous spot. This is destructive to the glass, obviously, but it’s the most definitive non-lab test available.

6. The price check (boring but accurate)

Natural amethyst of gua sha quality — cut from a single piece, polished to a continuous curve, no fractures along the stroke edge — costs money. On the current market, a legitimate amethyst gua sha sells for $15 to $60 depending on size, polish, and country of origin.

  • Under $10: almost always dyed glass or resin.
  • $10–$15: low-grade amethyst or quartzite dyed to look deeper purple.
  • $15–$35: legitimate everyday amethyst (this is where ours lives).
  • $35–$60+: museum-grade or large single-piece carvings.

You cannot mine, cut, polish, ship, and retail real amethyst for $6.99. The math doesn’t work.

Common fakes, and how they’re sold

Dyed glass

The most common fake. Molded into the familiar heart shape, then dyed purple during the glass melt. Looks convincing in photos, fails every physical test. Frequently sold as “amethyst glass” or “amethyst-infused” — both phrases meaning not amethyst.

Purple resin

Injection-molded plastic with pigment added. Extremely light, warms to body temperature the moment you touch it, and develops visible wear along the edge within a month of use. Also the most likely to chip and draw blood.

Dyed quartzite

The sneakiest fake because it’s actually stone — just not amethyst. Quartzite is a grainy metamorphic rock that takes dye well. It passes the temperature and weight tests, but the porous surface drags on skin, and the color washes out over a few months as the dye leaches into your serum.

“Enhanced” or heat-treated amethyst — real, but with an asterisk

Most commercial amethyst on the market today is mildly heat-treated to deepen the purple. This is still real amethyst, chemically identical to untreated stone, and the treatment is permanent. If you see a piece labeled “natural, untreated” it will cost more and usually show paler, more uneven color. Frequently asked questions

Does real amethyst actually scratch glass?

Yes. Amethyst sits at 7 on the Mohs scale, and standard soda-lime glass sits between 5.5 and 6. Harder minerals scratch softer ones, so a real amethyst edge will leave a visible, permanent mark on a glass bottle. If yours doesn’t, it’s not quartz.

Can a glass gua sha actually break the skin?

Unfortunately, yes — and this is our main frustration with cheap fakes. Molded glass chips along the edge with normal use, creating microscopic sharp points that can open small cuts along the jawline and cheekbone. Real polished amethyst wears down smoothly because the whole piece has uniform hardness; glass fakes fracture unevenly.

How should I store my amethyst gua sha to avoid chipping or fading?

Store it flat in its pouch, never standing on a narrow edge. Amethyst is hard but brittle — a drop onto a tile floor will chip a real one just as easily as a fake. Keep it out of direct sunlight for long periods; the purple color can fade over years of UV exposure, the same way sun bleaches curtains. Rinse with lukewarm water and pat dry after each use.

What’s the difference between amethyst and lavender quartz?

They’re cousins, not twins. Both are silicon dioxide (SiO₂) and both sit at Mohs 7, but amethyst gets its purple from iron impurities and natural irradiation, while lavender quartz (also called “lavender amethyst” by some sellers) is a much paler pink-violet variety colored by manganese and titanium. Lavender quartz is softer in color and usually cheaper. It’s still real stone and still works beautifully — just don’t pay amethyst prices for it.

Will a real amethyst gua sha feel cold in a warm room?

Yes. Because quartz conducts heat away from your skin faster than your skin can warm it, real amethyst feels cold even at 24°C ambient temperature. You don’t need to refrigerate it for the cooling effect — though many of us do, and it feels incredible.

What if my stone passes some tests but fails others?

Trust the failures. Fakes have gotten sophisticated enough to pass one or two tests (dyed quartzite will pass temperature and weight; thick glass can fool the bubble test if poured carefully). But no fake material passes all six. If you see a perfectly round bubble inside the stone, or the color is Pantone-uniform, or the tool weighed 38 grams — it doesn’t matter what else it passed. It’s not real amethyst.
