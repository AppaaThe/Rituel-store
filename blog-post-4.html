What Is Gua Sha?

Gua sha is a technique from Traditional Chinese Medicine that uses a smooth, flat tool to apply gentle pressure and directional strokes across the skin. The name translates roughly to "scraping" — though the facial version is far gentler than that word suggests.

Used on the body for centuries to treat pain and inflammation, the facial adaptation focuses on three specific effects: moving lymphatic fluid (the fluid that causes morning puffiness), stimulating blood circulation, and releasing tension in the muscles and connective tissue beneath the skin.

The result — when done consistently and correctly — is a face that looks less puffy, more defined, and healthier. Not because anything was added or injected, but because what was already there got optimised.

A 2007 clinical study found that gua sha increases microcirculation by up to 400% in the treated area. That's the science behind the visible effect. More blood flow means more oxygen, more lymphatic drainage, and better delivery of nutrients to skin cells.

What You Actually Need

You need three things. Not ten. Not a twelve-step system. Three.

🪨

A Gua Sha Stone

Natural stone with a curved, concave edge. The shape matters more than the material — it needs to fit the angle of your jaw and cheekbone. Amethyst is a good choice: dense, smooth, and stays cool against the skin.

🫙

Facial Oil

Non-negotiable. 2–3 drops of rosehip oil or a quality facial oil. The stone must glide — without oil it drags, which causes irritation and broken capillaries. This is the most important thing beginners get wrong.

🧊

Ice Roller (Optional)

Not essential, but it completes the ritual. Used immediately after gua sha, it seals in the sculpting effect, closes pores, and visibly reduces redness. The step that makes people ask what you had done.

That's it. You don't need a special cleanser, a specific moisturiser, or any other add-ons to get started. Master the three-item ritual first.

Your First Session: Step by Step

Your first session should take about 5 minutes. Don't overthink it. The technique is simple once you understand the two rules: always use oil, and always stroke upward.

01

Start Clean

Wash your face and pat dry. Gua sha works on bare skin — not over SPF, not over makeup, not over last night's moisturiser. Clean skin only.

02

Apply Oil

Put 2–3 drops of facial oil in your palm. Press both palms together to warm it, then press gently into your face — jaw, neck, cheeks. The stone should glide effortlessly. If it catches or drags, add more oil.

03

Start at the Neck

Always start here. Your lymph nodes are in your neck — you need to open this drainage pathway before pushing fluid toward it. 5–6 upward strokes from collarbone to jaw on each side. Light pressure.

04

Work the Jawline

Hold the stone almost flat against your skin (15–30 degree angle). Use the curved edge. Start at your chin, sweep outward and upward toward your ear. 5–8 strokes per section. Always toward the ear, never downward. Finish one side before moving to the other.

05

Cheekbones

From the side of your nose, sweep outward along the cheekbone toward your temple. 5–8 strokes. This is where the most visible sculpting happens for most people.

06

Forehead

Sweep from center outward toward the hairline, then upward from brows. 5–6 strokes in each direction. Light pressure — the forehead is bony and needs less work than the jaw.

07

Ice Roller (If You Have One)

Roll immediately after gua sha for 60–90 seconds. The cold constricts blood vessels, seals the sculpted effect, and reduces any redness from the session. This is the finishing step.

Questions Every Beginner Asks

Does it hurt?

No — and if it does, you're pressing too hard. Facial gua sha uses very light pressure. The stone should feel like it's gliding across your skin, not dragging through it. If you feel discomfort, reduce pressure immediately.

How often should I do it?

Daily, ideally every morning. This is when your face is most puffy and the drainage effect is most visible. Once you see results, you'll understand why morning is the right time.

Can I do it at night?

Yes. Evening gua sha is great for relaxing jaw tension and prepping your skin to absorb night serums more effectively. The same rules apply — clean skin, oil first, upward strokes.

Will it break me out?

Gua sha itself doesn't cause breakouts. However, avoid doing it over active pimples — the mechanical stimulation can spread bacteria. Work around any active spots and resume normal technique once they've healed.

My face is red after — is that normal?

Slight redness immediately after is normal and fades within 10–15 minutes — it's just increased blood flow. Persistent redness or irritation means you used too much pressure. Reduce it at your next session.

How do I clean the stone?

Wash it with mild soap and warm water after each use. Pat dry with a soft cloth. Store it somewhere it won't get knocked around — natural stone can chip if dropped on a hard surface.

Does it matter which stone I use?

The shape matters more than the material. You need a concave edge that fits your jaw angle — a flat stone won't give you the same contact. For material, choose natural stone over plastic. It stays cooler, has the right weight, and glides better.
